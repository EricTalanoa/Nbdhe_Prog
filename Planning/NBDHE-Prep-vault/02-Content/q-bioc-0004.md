# Stem
Following a sucrose rinse, plaque pH drops sharply and then returns to baseline over roughly
30–40 minutes. Which factor most directly worsens the cariogenic effect of this pH drop?

# Options
- A) the total daily caloric intake from carbohydrates
- B) the frequency of fermentable carbohydrate exposures per day (correct)
- C) whether the carbohydrate is consumed with a meal or alone
- D) the fiber content of the food consumed

# Correct answer
B

# Rationale
Each fermentable-carbohydrate exposure triggers a Stephan curve — a rapid plaque pH drop below the
critical pH (~5.5) followed by a slow recovery. Frequent exposures keep plaque pH depressed for a
larger share of the day, shortening remineralization time and driving net demineralization, which
is why exposure frequency (not total sugar amount) is the strongest dietary predictor of caries
risk.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- A) Total caloric or sugar quantity matters less than how often the sugar is introduced across the
  day; a large single exposure produces one Stephan curve, not many.
- C) Eating fermentable carbohydrate with a meal (versus alone as a snack) tends to blunt the pH
  drop via increased salivary flow and buffering, so this describes a protective timing factor, not
  the mechanism that worsens the curve.
- D) Fiber can increase salivary flow and mechanical clearance, which is protective, but it is not
  the factor that most directly worsens the pH drop itself.
