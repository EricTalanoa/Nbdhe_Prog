# Stem
A patient's medical history lists a tricyclic antidepressant (e.g., amitriptyline). When planning
local anesthesia with a vasoconstrictor for this patient, the most appropriate approach is to:

# Options
- A) administer a standard dental cartridge concentration of epinephrine (e.g., 1:100,000), aspirate carefully before injecting, and avoid epinephrine-impregnated gingival retraction cord (correct)
- B) withhold all local anesthesia until the patient's physician discontinues the tricyclic antidepressant
- C) use a local anesthetic with epinephrine at twice the usual concentration to ensure adequate hemostasis
- D) select a local anesthetic containing norepinephrine instead of epinephrine, since norepinephrine is unaffected by tricyclic antidepressants

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Tricyclic antidepressants block neuronal reuptake of norepinephrine, which can potentiate the
cardiovascular (pressor) effects of exogenously administered epinephrine and, especially,
norepinephrine. In the small doses delivered in standard dental cartridges, this interaction is
managed safely with careful technique — aspirating before injection, injecting slowly, and using
the lowest effective epinephrine concentration. The much higher epinephrine concentration in
gingival retraction cord makes that specific product the item to avoid in these patients.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Discontinuing a patient's psychiatric medication is not within the hygienist's scope and is
  unnecessary; normal dental doses of vasoconstrictor are used safely with careful technique.
- C) Increasing the epinephrine concentration or dose worsens the interaction risk rather than
  managing it; standard concentrations and minimal effective doses are used instead.
- D) Norepinephrine's pressor effect is potentiated by tricyclic antidepressants even more than
  epinephrine's, so substituting norepinephrine increases risk rather than avoiding it.
