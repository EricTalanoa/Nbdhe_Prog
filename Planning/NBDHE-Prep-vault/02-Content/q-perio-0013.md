# Stem
Subantimicrobial-dose doxycycline (SDD), prescribed as an adjunct to scaling and root planing,
works by:

# Options
- A) directly killing periodontal pathogens at the low dose prescribed
- B) inhibiting host-derived matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) that break down connective tissue and bone (correct)
- C) selectively suppressing only gram-negative anaerobes
- D) forming a substantive antimicrobial film over the root surface

# Correct answer
B

# Rationale
At the subantimicrobial dose (20 mg twice daily) used for host modulation, doxycycline does not
reach levels that kill bacteria. Instead it inhibits collagenase and other host-derived matrix
metalloproteinases, reducing the connective tissue and bone breakdown driven by the host's own
inflammatory response — an adjunct to, not a replacement for, mechanical debridement.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- A) Antibacterial killing requires antimicrobial dosing; SDD is deliberately dosed below that
  threshold precisely to avoid an antibacterial effect (and the resistance risk that comes with it).
- C) SDD's mechanism is host-directed (anti-collagenase), not a selective antibacterial action
  against any bacterial subgroup.
- D) Substantive antimicrobial films describe agents like chlorhexidine, not a systemic host
  modulation drug.
