# Stem
Given this patient's xerostomia and new root-surface caries, which fluoride recommendation is most
appropriate?

# Options
- A) daily home use of a prescription-strength 5,000 ppm fluoride toothpaste (correct)
- B) an in-office fluoride varnish once, with no further follow-up needed
- C) switching to an over-the-counter 1,100 ppm fluoride toothpaste used twice weekly
- D) an acidulated phosphate fluoride rinse used once a month

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Patients with xerostomia and active root caries benefit most from a daily high-concentration
(5,000 ppm) prescription fluoride dentifrice, which provides continuous topical exposure to
remineralize root surfaces that are especially vulnerable once cementum and dentin are exposed by
recession. This is standard of care for xerostomia-related caries risk and is easy for a patient
with limited dexterity to work into an existing brushing routine.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) A single in-office varnish application gives short-term benefit but is not sufficient alone
  for a patient with ongoing high caries risk from chronic dry mouth; it should supplement, not
  replace, daily home fluoride.
- C) Standard 1,100 ppm toothpaste used less than daily is a step down in both concentration and
  frequency from what this high-risk patient needs.
- D) Monthly professional rinse use is far too infrequent to counter continuous demineralization in
  a chronically hyposalivary mouth.
