# Stem
When planning pain control for a patient with a documented true allergy to ester-type local
anesthetics, the most appropriate choice of pretopical anesthetic before an amide injection is a
topical agent from the:

# Options
- A) ester class, at a lower concentration to reduce allergen exposure
- B) amide class, such as lidocaine, since true cross-sensitivity between ester and amide classes is not expected (correct)
- C) ester class, but only after premedicating with an oral antihistamine
- D) neither class; pretopical anesthesia should be skipped entirely for this patient

# Correct answer
B

# Rationale
Ester and amide local anesthetics are metabolized differently and do not share the allergenic
mechanism responsible for most reported "anesthetic allergies" (which is more often the
para-aminobenzoic acid metabolite of esters, or sulfite preservatives in vasoconstrictor
solutions). A patient with a true ester allergy can safely use an amide topical, such as
lidocaine, before an amide injection.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- A) Using any ester-class agent, even diluted, still exposes an allergic patient to the class of
  concern and is unnecessary when a safe amide alternative exists.
- C) Premedicating with an antihistamine to "cover" a known allergen exposure is not an accepted
  substitute for simply avoiding the allergen.
- D) Skipping pretopical anesthesia denies the patient an established comfort measure that is
  unnecessary to withhold once a safe amide topical is available.
