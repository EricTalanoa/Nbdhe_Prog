# Stem
During a periodontal maintenance visit, a hygienist evaluates a patient's dental implant for
peri-implant health. All of the following are appropriate elements of implant maintenance EXCEPT:

# Options
- A) probing peri-implant tissues with light force using a plastic or implant-safe (e.g., titanium or graphite-composite) probe
- B) instrumenting the implant abutment surface with a conventional stainless-steel curette (correct)
- C) reviewing periapical radiographs over time to monitor for peri-implant crestal bone loss
- D) assessing for bleeding on probing and suppuration as signs of peri-implant mucositis or peri-implantitis

# Correct answer
B

# Rationale
Stainless-steel and other metal instruments are harder than the titanium (or titanium-alloy)
implant surface and can scratch it, creating micro-grooves that increase biofilm retention and
raise the risk of peri-implantitis. Instrumentation should use implant-safe materials such as
plastic, resin-coated, or titanium instruments instead.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- A) A plastic or implant-safe probe with light force is the recommended method for assessing
  peri-implant probing depths without damaging the implant surface.
- C) Serial radiographs are a standard way to track peri-implant bone levels over time.
- D) Bleeding on probing and suppuration are key clinical signs used to identify peri-implant
  mucositis or peri-implantitis.
