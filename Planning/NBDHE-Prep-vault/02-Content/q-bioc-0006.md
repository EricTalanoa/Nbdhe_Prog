# Stem
A patient presents with angular cheilitis (cracking at the corners of the mouth) and a smooth, red,
atrophic tongue with loss of the filiform papillae. These oral findings are most classically
associated with a deficiency of which nutrients?

# Options
- A) vitamin C
- B) B-complex vitamins and iron (correct)
- C) vitamin D and calcium
- D) vitamin K

# Correct answer
B

# Rationale
The oral mucosa and tongue have a high epithelial turnover, so they show early signs of deficiency
in the nutrients needed for cell renewal and hemoglobin synthesis. Deficiencies of B-complex
vitamins (riboflavin, niacin, folate, B12) and of iron classically produce angular cheilitis,
glossitis, and an atrophic ("bald," red) tongue from loss of the filiform papillae — findings the
hygienist may be the first to notice.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- A) Vitamin C deficiency (scurvy) impairs collagen synthesis and characteristically causes
  swollen, spongy, bleeding gingiva — not the atrophic-tongue/angular-cheilitis picture.
- C) Vitamin D and calcium deficiency affects mineralization of bone and teeth (e.g., rickets,
  osteomalacia); it is not the classic cause of glossitis or angular cheilitis.
- D) Vitamin K is required for clotting-factor synthesis; its deficiency causes bleeding
  tendencies, not atrophic glossitis or cracking at the labial commissures.
