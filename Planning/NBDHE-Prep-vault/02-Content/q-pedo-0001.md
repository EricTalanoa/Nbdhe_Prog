# Stem
The caries pattern in this child — cavitation on the maxillary incisors with the mandibular
incisors spared — is most consistent with:

# Options
- A) early childhood caries from frequent bottle and cup exposure (correct)
- B) an inherited enamel formation defect
- C) dental fluorosis from excess fluoride
- D) trauma to the anterior teeth

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
This is the classic early-childhood-caries (nursing/bottle caries) pattern: frequent, prolonged
exposure to fermentable carbohydrate (milk, juice) — especially at sleep, when salivary flow falls
— demineralizes the maxillary incisors first. During sucking the tongue covers and protects the
mandibular incisors, so they are relatively spared. Management centers on reducing exposure
frequency, weaning from the bedtime bottle, and fluoride.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Inherited enamel defects (e.g., amelogenesis imperfecta) affect the dentition symmetrically,
  not in this bottle-caries distribution.
- C) Fluorosis is diffuse and this community lacks water fluoridation, making excess fluoride
  unlikely.
- D) Trauma would not produce this bilateral, surface-specific caries pattern.
