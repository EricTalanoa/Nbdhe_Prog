# Stem
The exposed, non-healing bone at this patient's extraction site is most consistent with which
condition, and what is the appropriate hygiene-care implication?

# Options
- A) medication-related osteonecrosis of the jaw (MRONJ); continue nonsurgical hygiene care and refer for management, while avoiding elective bone-invasive procedures (correct)
- B) a normal healing socket that simply needs more time and no referral
- C) a routine dry socket that will resolve with a sedative dressing and no further concern
- D) periodontal abscess best treated by immediately extracting the adjacent teeth

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Exposed bone in the maxillofacial region that fails to heal within **8 weeks** in a patient on
antiresorptive therapy, with no history of head-and-neck radiation, defines **MRONJ**.
Antiresorptive drugs suppress osteoclastic bone turnover, so bone-invasive procedures (especially
extractions) can leave bone that does not remodel or heal. The hygiene implications: **nonsurgical
care — scaling, oral-hygiene instruction, prevention — is safe and encouraged** to keep the patient
healthy and reduce the future need for extractions, while the necrotic site is **referred** for
appropriate management; elective bone-invasive procedures are avoided or coordinated with the
physician/surgeon.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Eight weeks with exposed rough bone is not normal healing; ignoring it misses a diagnosis that
  needs referral and altered planning.
- C) A dry socket (alveolar osteitis) is an early post-extraction pain syndrome that resolves in
  days to a couple of weeks; it does not present as persistently exposed bone at 8 weeks in an
  antiresorptive patient.
- D) This is not a periodontal abscess, and extracting adjacent teeth would introduce **more**
  bone-invasive trauma — exactly what raises MRONJ risk.
