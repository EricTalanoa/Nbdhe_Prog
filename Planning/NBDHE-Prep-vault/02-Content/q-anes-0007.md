# Stem
A local anesthetic with a pKa nearer to physiologic pH generally has a faster onset because a
greater proportion of the drug is present as the:

# Options
- A) uncharged base that diffuses across the nerve membrane (correct)
- B) charged cation that remains outside the nerve
- C) protein-bound fraction circulating in plasma
- D) inactive metabolite produced in the liver

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Onset depends on how much anesthetic can cross the lipid nerve membrane. Only the uncharged
(nonionized) base is lipid-soluble enough to diffuse through; the closer the pKa is to tissue pH,
the larger that base fraction, so onset is faster. Once inside the axon, the molecule re-equilibrates
and it is the charged cation that binds the sodium channel to block conduction.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) The charged cation cannot cross the membrane; although it is the form that ultimately blocks
  the sodium channel from inside, its extracellular abundance slows, not speeds, diffusion.
- C) Protein binding correlates with duration of action, not speed of onset.
- D) Hepatic metabolites are inactive and unrelated to onset.
