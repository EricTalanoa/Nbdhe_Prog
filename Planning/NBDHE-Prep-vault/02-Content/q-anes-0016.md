# Stem
Prilocaine and, to a lesser extent, articaine carry a dose-dependent risk of which adverse effect,
caused by a metabolite that oxidizes the iron in hemoglobin and impairs its oxygen-carrying
capacity?

# Options
- A) Methemoglobinemia (correct)
- B) Malignant hyperthermia
- C) Status epilepticus
- D) Agranulocytosis

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Prilocaine is metabolized to o-toluidine, which oxidizes hemoglobin's ferrous iron (Fe2+) to the
ferric state (Fe3+), forming methemoglobin that cannot carry oxygen; articaine carries a smaller
version of this same risk. At high cumulative doses this can produce clinically significant
methemoglobinemia — presenting as cyanosis that does not improve with supplemental oxygen — so
total dose is limited, especially in patients who are anemic, have cardiopulmonary disease, are
very young, or are taking other methemoglobin-inducing drugs.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Malignant hyperthermia is triggered by volatile inhalation anesthetics and succinylcholine,
  not by amide local anesthetics such as prilocaine or articaine.
- C) Status epilepticus can theoretically follow severe local anesthetic CNS toxicity from an
  intravascular overdose, but that is a generic overdose effect, not the specific dose-dependent
  mechanism described (an oxidized-hemoglobin metabolite).
- D) Agranulocytosis is a drug-induced blood dyscrasia associated with certain other medications,
  not a recognized effect of prilocaine or articaine metabolism.
