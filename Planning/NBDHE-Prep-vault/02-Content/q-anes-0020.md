# Stem
A local anesthetic's duration of action correlates most closely with its degree of:

# Options
- A) protein binding, since a more highly protein-bound drug attaches more firmly to the sodium channel protein and is released more slowly (correct)
- B) lipid solubility, since this property primarily governs how quickly the drug diffuses into the nerve membrane and takes effect
- C) pKa, since a lower pKa yields a higher proportion of the un-ionized base and a longer-lasting block
- D) vasodilating potency, since a more strongly vasodilating agent is cleared from the injection site fastest and therefore acts longest

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Duration of action correlates with protein binding: local anesthetics act by binding to protein
receptor sites on the voltage-gated sodium channel, and an agent with a higher percentage of
protein binding (e.g., bupivacaine, ~95%) attaches more firmly and is released more slowly, giving
a longer clinical duration than a lower-protein-binding agent (e.g., procaine). This is a distinct
pharmacologic property from potency and onset speed.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Lipid solubility governs potency (how readily the drug crosses the lipid-rich nerve membrane),
  not duration of action.
- C) pKa governs onset speed, not duration: a pKa closer to physiologic pH increases the un-ionized
  free-base fraction available to diffuse into the nerve, producing a faster onset — but this is
  unrelated to how long the block lasts once established.
- D) Greater vasodilation increases blood flow at the injection site, which washes the anesthetic
  away faster and shortens, not lengthens, duration — this is why vasoconstrictors are added to
  extend duration.
