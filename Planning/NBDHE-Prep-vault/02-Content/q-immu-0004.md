# Stem
A functional defect in neutrophil (PMN) chemotaxis is implicated in patients who show unusually
rapid, severe periodontal attachment loss despite relatively low plaque and calculus levels. This
defect most directly impairs which host-defense function at the gingival sulcus?

# Options
- A) The ability of neutrophils to migrate toward and phagocytose subgingival bacteria (correct)
- B) The production of secretory IgA by plasma cells in the gingival connective tissue
- C) The synthesis of collagen by gingival fibroblasts
- D) The activation of osteoblasts to form new bone

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Neutrophils are the first-line phagocytic defense within the gingival crevice. A chemotactic or
adhesion defect prevents PMNs from migrating efficiently to and engulfing subgingival pathogens,
allowing pathogenic species to proliferate unchecked. This mechanism is implicated in the rapid,
severe attachment loss seen with minimal plaque/calculus in some cases of aggressive
periodontitis.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Secretory IgA production is a humoral/adaptive immune function carried out by plasma cells,
  not a neutrophil function affected by a chemotactic defect.
- C) Fibroblast collagen synthesis is a connective-tissue repair function, unrelated to neutrophil
  migration or phagocytosis.
- D) Osteoblast activation drives bone *formation*; it is not a phagocytic host-defense function
  and is not what a neutrophil chemotaxis defect impairs.
