# Stem
A triclosan/copolymer dentifrice reduces plaque and gingival inflammation primarily because the
copolymer:

# Options
- A) binds triclosan to tooth and soft-tissue surfaces, extending its retention time so the antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory effects last well beyond brushing (correct)
- B) mechanically abrades biofilm from the tooth surface, with triclosan present only to flavor the dentifrice
- C) forms a calcium-binding complex that remineralizes enamel, independent of any antimicrobial action
- D) neutralizes salivary pH to prevent demineralization, which secondarily reduces bacterial colonization

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Triclosan alone is rapidly cleared from the oral cavity after brushing. Copolymer (a polyvinyl
methyl ether/maleic acid copolymer) adsorbs to dental and mucosal surfaces and binds triclosan to
it, substantially prolonging triclosan's substantivity. This extended retention lets triclosan's
broad-spectrum antibacterial action and its inhibition of the arachidonic acid/cyclooxygenase
pathway (reducing prostaglandin-mediated inflammation) continue working between brushings, giving
the combination measurable reductions in plaque, gingivitis, and calculus versus a plain dentifrice.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Mechanical abrasion from brushing contributes to plaque removal regardless of dentifrice
  formulation, but the triclosan/copolymer combination's benefit comes specifically from the
  copolymer's substantivity effect on the antimicrobial agent, not from added abrasiveness.
- C) Remineralization is a fluoride-driven effect on enamel; it is a separate mechanism unrelated to
  the copolymer's role of retaining triclosan on oral surfaces.
- D) The formulation's benefit is antimicrobial/anti-inflammatory substantivity, not salivary pH
  buffering.
