# Stem
A patient completed active periodontal therapy but continues to smoke and still has several
residual 5-mm pockets. Compared with a low-risk, non-smoking patient who has no residual deep
pockets, this patient's periodontal maintenance recall interval should be:

# Options
- A) Shortened to about every 3 months, since smoking and residual deep pockets both raise the risk of disease recurrence (correct)
- B) Left at a standard 6-month interval, since recall frequency is not adjusted for individual risk factors
- C) Lengthened beyond 6 months, since active therapy has already been completed
- D) Set only by insurance coverage limits, not by clinical risk factors

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Supportive periodontal therapy (maintenance) intervals are individualized by risk: patients with
modifiable risk factors like smoking, plus anatomic risk factors like residual deep pockets, are
more likely to have disease recurrence and progression, so they are typically recalled every 3–4
months rather than the standard 6 months used for low-risk, fully resolved patients.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Recall frequency is explicitly risk-based in periodontal maintenance protocols — a one-size
  interval ignores the patient's elevated recurrence risk.
- C) Completing active therapy does not eliminate ongoing risk; unresolved risk factors (smoking,
  residual pockets) argue for closer, not longer, follow-up.
- D) Clinical risk, not insurance policy, should drive the recommended recall interval, even though
  coverage may separately affect what the patient can afford.
