# Stem
Immediately after an instrument causes a percutaneous sharps injury during patient care, the
dental hygienist's first and most appropriate action is to:

# Options
- A) wash the wound with soap and water, then report the exposure per the office's post-exposure protocol for evaluation and possible follow-up (correct)
- B) apply bleach or another caustic disinfectant directly to the puncture site to kill any pathogens
- C) finish the current procedure on the patient first, then address the wound once the appointment ends
- D) apply a bandage and continue working without reporting, since minor sharps injuries do not require follow-up

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Current CDC/OSHA guidance for a percutaneous exposure is to immediately wash the wound with soap
and water (mucous membranes are flushed with water), then report the exposure right away so the
facility's post-exposure protocol can proceed — source-patient testing, risk assessment, and
timely post-exposure prophylaxis if indicated, since prophylaxis effectiveness is time-sensitive.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Caustic agents like bleach are not recommended for wound care; they can damage tissue and do
  not replace the standard wash-and-report protocol.
- C) Exposure management should begin immediately, not be deferred until a procedure is finished;
  delay reduces the effectiveness of timely risk assessment and any indicated prophylaxis.
- D) All percutaneous sharps injuries require reporting under OSHA's Bloodborne Pathogens Standard,
  regardless of how minor the injury appears, so the exposure can be properly evaluated.
