# Stem
Compared with traditional sodium bicarbonate air polishing, glycine powder air polishing is
preferred for use on exposed root surfaces and around implant abutments because glycine:

# Options
- A) has smaller, rounder particles that are less abrasive to root dentin and titanium surfaces (correct)
- B) contains an antimicrobial agent that eliminates the need for subsequent instrumentation
- C) is more abrasive, allowing faster removal of calculus deposits
- D) cannot be used supragingivally, only subgingivally

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Glycine is a naturally occurring amino acid formulated into a fine, round-particle powder that is
substantially less abrasive than sodium bicarbonate. This lower hardness and smaller particle size
make it safer for subgingival use on root surfaces and around implant/abutment surfaces, where
bicarbonate's coarser, harder crystals can cause excessive substrate loss or surface roughening.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Glycine powder is not antimicrobial; it is a mechanical biofilm-removal adjunct, not a
  replacement for instrumentation.
- C) Glycine is less abrasive than bicarbonate, not more; its safety profile comes from lower
  hardness, not faster cutting.
- D) Glycine powder can be used both supra- and subgingivally (with a subgingival-rated nozzle);
  it is not restricted to subgingival use only.
