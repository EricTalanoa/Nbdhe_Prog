# Stem
The "red complex" periodontal pathogens (e.g., *Porphyromonas gingivalis*) are gram-negative
anaerobes. Which structural component of their cell wall most directly triggers the host
inflammatory response that drives periodontal tissue destruction?

# Options
- A) Lipopolysaccharide (LPS/endotoxin) (correct)
- B) Teichoic acid
- C) Peptidoglycan-associated lipoprotein of gram-positive cocci
- D) Flagellin from motile enteric bacteria

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Lipopolysaccharide, found in the outer membrane of gram-negative bacteria, is a potent endotoxin
recognized by host pattern-recognition receptors (e.g., TLR4) on macrophages and monocytes. This
recognition triggers release of proinflammatory cytokines (IL-1, TNF-α) and matrix
metalloproteinases that drive the collagen and alveolar bone breakdown seen in periodontitis.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Teichoic acid is a component of gram-*positive* cell walls, not the gram-negative red-complex
  pathogens described.
- C) This describes a gram-positive structure, irrelevant to the gram-negative organisms in the
  stem.
- D) Flagellin can trigger innate immune receptors in some organisms, but it is not the
  characteristic trigger implicated in red-complex-driven periodontal destruction — these species
  are not primarily recognized for flagellar motility.
