# Stem
Based on a caries-risk assessment (e.g., CAMBRA) of this patient, what is his caries risk level?

# Options
- A) high (correct)
- B) low
- C) moderate
- D) risk cannot be assigned without a salivary bacterial culture

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
In risk-assessment models such as CAMBRA, **any cavitated or new caries lesion places the patient
in the high-risk category**, and this patient has multiple cavitated coronal and root-surface
lesions. He also carries several disease indicators and risk factors that reinforce that level:
reduced salivary flow (xerostomia), frequent daily sugar exposures (sweetened coffee and soda), and
poor plaque control. The management plan should match a high-risk patient — high-concentration
fluoride, salivary support, and diet counseling — not a routine protocol.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Low risk requires no risk factors and no disease; the presence of active cavitated lesions
  alone rules this out.
- C) Moderate risk applies when risk factors exist **without** active cavitation; here the active
  lesions plus xerostomia and high sugar frequency push the patient into the high category.
- D) Caries risk is assigned clinically from disease indicators, risk factors, and protective
  factors; a bacterial culture is not required to classify risk.
