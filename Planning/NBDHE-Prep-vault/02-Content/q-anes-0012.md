# Stem
True allergic reactions to local anesthetics occur far more often with ester-type agents than
amide-type agents primarily because ester metabolism produces:

# Options
- A) para-aminobenzoic acid (PABA), a well-documented allergen (correct)
- B) methemoglobin, which triggers an IgE-mediated response
- C) epinephrine breakdown products that cross-react with the immune system
- D) a byproduct identical to penicillin, provoking cross-reactivity in penicillin-allergic patients

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Esters (e.g., procaine, benzocaine) are hydrolyzed by plasma pseudocholinesterase into metabolites
that include PABA, a recognized allergen and sensitizer. This is why documented true allergy is
almost always tied to ester agents. Amides (e.g., lidocaine, articaine) are metabolized mainly in
the liver and do not produce PABA, so genuine amide allergy is rare — many reported "allergies" to
amides are actually reactions to bisulfite preservatives, vasoconstrictor effects, or vasovagal
episodes.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Methemoglobinemia is a dose-related toxic effect of certain agents (e.g., prilocaine,
  benzocaine), not an immune-mediated allergic mechanism.
- C) Epinephrine metabolism is unrelated to the ester-vs-amide allergy distinction.
- D) Ester metabolites are not chemically related to penicillin; there is no expected
  cross-reactivity with penicillin allergy.
