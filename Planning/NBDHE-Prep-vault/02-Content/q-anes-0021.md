# Stem
A patient takes a nonselective beta-adrenergic blocker (e.g., propranolol) for hypertension. What
is the primary concern with using a local anesthetic containing epinephrine for this patient?

# Options
- A) unopposed alpha-receptor stimulation can cause a significant rise in blood pressure with reflex bradycardia (correct)
- B) the beta-blocker will completely inactivate the local anesthetic, so profound anesthesia cannot be achieved
- C) epinephrine will reverse the antihypertensive effect by directly blocking the beta receptor
- D) the interaction only matters with articaine and does not apply to other amide anesthetics

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Nonselective beta-blockers block both beta-1 (cardiac) and beta-2 (vascular smooth muscle,
mediating vasodilation) receptors. When epinephrine is given to a patient on a nonselective
beta-blocker, its beta-2 vasodilatory effect is blocked while its alpha-mediated
vasoconstriction is unopposed, which can produce a significant rise in blood pressure and a
compensatory (reflex) bradycardia. Aspiration and use of the lowest effective epinephrine dose are
recommended; this differs from the tricyclic-antidepressant interaction, where the concern is
potentiation of epinephrine's pressor effect through a different mechanism.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Beta-blockers do not inactivate local anesthetic molecules or block their sodium-channel
  mechanism; anesthesia is still achieved.
- C) Epinephrine acts on adrenergic receptors as an agonist; it does not competitively block beta
  receptors or reverse the antihypertensive drug's mechanism.
- D) The unopposed alpha-stimulation interaction applies to epinephrine itself, regardless of
  which local anesthetic (lidocaine, articaine, mepivacaine, etc.) it is combined with.
