# Stem
Drug-induced gingival enlargement (associated with phenytoin, cyclosporine, or nifedipine) is
classified as gingival hyperplasia rather than hypertrophy because the tissue increase results
from:

# Options
- A) an increase in the number of fibroblasts and accumulation of extracellular matrix (correct)
- B) an increase in the size of existing cells without any new cell formation
- C) replacement of gingival connective tissue with dense fibrous scar
- D) infiltration of the tissue by malignant cells

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Hyperplasia is defined as an increase in the *number* of cells in a tissue. Drug-induced gingival
overgrowth results from an increase in fibroblast number along with accumulation of collagen and
ground substance — the mechanism behind the enlargement seen with phenytoin, cyclosporine, and
nifedipine.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) This describes hypertrophy — an increase in the *size* of existing cells without new cell
  formation — the opposite cellular mechanism from what occurs here.
- C) This describes fibrosis/scarring following tissue injury, not the proliferative overgrowth
  caused by these medications.
- D) This describes neoplastic infiltration, an unrelated and far more serious process than a
  benign drug reaction.
