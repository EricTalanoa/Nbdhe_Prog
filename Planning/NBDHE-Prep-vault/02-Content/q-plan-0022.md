# Stem
Within minutes of receiving a local anesthetic injection, a patient develops facial and lip
swelling, generalized hives, and audible wheezing, then reports feeling faint. The most appropriate
immediate management is to:

# Options
- A) position the patient supine with legs elevated, activate EMS, and administer epinephrine IM in the anterolateral thigh (correct)
- B) have the patient rinse with cool water and observe for 15 minutes before deciding on further care
- C) administer the patient's short-acting bronchodilator inhaler only, since the wheezing is the priority
- D) give an oral antihistamine tablet and reappoint the patient for a later date

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
This presentation — rapid-onset skin/mucosal swelling and hives with respiratory involvement
(wheezing) and a sign of impending shock (faintness) — is anaphylaxis, a life-threatening
emergency. First-line treatment is prompt intramuscular epinephrine (anterolateral thigh, mid-
outer thigh muscle), positioning the patient to support blood pressure (supine with legs elevated
if tolerated), activating emergency medical services, and administering oxygen while monitoring
airway, breathing, and circulation.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) A "wait and observe" approach delays the one intervention (epinephrine) that reverses
  anaphylaxis and can allow rapid progression to airway obstruction or cardiovascular collapse.
- C) A bronchodilator alone does not address the systemic mast-cell-mediated reaction or the risk
  of hypotension and airway swelling; epinephrine is required first.
- D) Oral antihistamines act too slowly and address only histamine-mediated skin symptoms, not the
  acute cardiorespiratory threat of anaphylaxis; the patient cannot simply be rescheduled.
