# Stem
Compared with a conventional two-dimensional panoramic radiograph, cone-beam computed tomography
(CBCT) primarily offers the clinician:

# Options
- A) a three-dimensional volumetric image with no radiation exposure at all
- B) a three-dimensional volumetric image, generally at a higher patient dose than a panoramic (correct)
- C) a two-dimensional image with improved contrast but the same field of view
- D) real-time fluoroscopic imaging of the temporomandibular joint

# Correct answer
B

# Rationale
CBCT reconstructs a three-dimensional volume from a single rotation of the source and a
flat-panel detector, letting the clinician view structures (impacted teeth, implant sites, the
TMJ) in any plane. That extra diagnostic information comes at an effective dose that is higher
than a panoramic image, though still well below a medical fan-beam CT, so its use should be
limited to cases where a 2-D image is insufficient.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- A) CBCT still uses ionizing radiation; "no exposure at all" is never true of an X-ray-based
  technique.
- C) CBCT's advantage is the 3-D volume, not a same-field-of-view 2-D image.
- D) CBCT captures a static volumetric dataset, not continuous real-time fluoroscopy.
