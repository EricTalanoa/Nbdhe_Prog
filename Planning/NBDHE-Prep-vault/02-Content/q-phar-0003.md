# Stem
Prescribing a bacteriostatic antibiotic (e.g., tetracycline) together with a bactericidal
antibiotic that requires actively dividing bacteria to be effective (e.g., penicillin) can result
in a pharmacologic interaction best described as:

# Options
- A) synergistic
- B) antagonistic (correct)
- C) additive
- D) idiosyncratic

# Correct answer
B

# Rationale
Bactericidal drugs like penicillins kill by disrupting cell-wall synthesis in actively multiplying
bacteria. A bacteriostatic drug like tetracycline halts bacterial growth and division, leaving
fewer actively dividing organisms for the bactericidal drug to act on — an antagonistic
interaction that can blunt the bactericidal drug's effectiveness.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- A) Synergistic describes a combination whose effect exceeds the sum of each drug's individual
  effect (e.g., pairing two bactericidal agents against certain periodontal pathogens) — the
  opposite of what happens when a bacteriostatic drug blunts a bactericidal drug's mechanism.
- C) An additive effect would mean the combined result simply equals the sum of each drug acting
  independently; reduced killing from halted bacterial division is a true antagonism, not a
  neutral additive outcome.
- D) Idiosyncratic reactions are unpredictable, patient-specific responses unrelated to dose or
  mechanism, not a category describing a predictable pharmacologic drug-drug interaction.
