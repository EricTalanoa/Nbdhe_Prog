# Stem
The chalky white-spot lesions around this patient's brackets are intact and non-cavitated. What do
they represent, and what is the most appropriate management?

# Options
- A) Early subsurface demineralization that can be arrested and remineralized with improved plaque control and fluoride (correct)
- B) Full-thickness cavitated caries that require immediate restoration of all four teeth
- C) Permanent enamel scars that no intervention can affect, so nothing should be done
- D) Dental fluorosis from the fluoridated water, requiring the patient to stop drinking tap water

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
A white-spot lesion is **early enamel caries** — subsurface demineralization beneath a relatively
intact surface layer, appearing chalky/opaque because the porous enamel scatters light. Because the
surface is not yet cavitated, the lesion is **reversible**: shifting the demineralization–
remineralization balance toward remineralization (meticulous plaque removal around the appliances,
topical fluoride to drive fluorapatite formation, and reducing fermentable-carbohydrate frequency)
can arrest and partially reverse it. Non-cavitated lesions are managed medically, not with the
drill.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) The lesions are explicitly non-cavitated; restoring intact surfaces is over-treatment and
  removes sound tooth structure that could instead remineralize.
- C) White-spot lesions are not inert permanent scars at this stage — early, intact lesions respond
  to fluoride and plaque control; doing nothing risks progression to cavitation.
- D) The pattern (localized to bracket margins, with heavy local plaque) is caries-related
  demineralization, not fluorosis, which is a diffuse, symmetric developmental change; stopping
  optimally fluoridated water would remove a protective factor.
