# Stem
For a patient with significant cardiovascular disease (e.g., recent myocardial infarction, poorly
controlled hypertension), current guidance on local anesthetic with epinephrine 1:100,000 is to:

# Options
- A) limit epinephrine to no more than 0.04 mg (about two cartridges), aspirate before each injection, and inject slowly (correct)
- B) avoid all vasoconstrictors and use plain mepivacaine or plain prilocaine only, regardless of the procedure
- C) use a higher epinephrine concentration (1:50,000) to shorten total anesthetic exposure time
- D) there is no need to modify anesthetic technique in cardiovascular disease as long as the patient is asymptomatic that day

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Conservative, widely cited guidance (echoed by the ADA and AHA) caps epinephrine at roughly 0.04 mg
in patients with significant cardiovascular disease — about two cartridges of a 1:100,000
formulation — while still using slow injection and aspiration to minimize the chance of an
intravascular bolus. This allows the real benefits of a vasoconstrictor (deeper anesthesia, reduced
systemic uptake of the anesthetic, less need for supplemental injections) while limiting the
cardiovascular stimulant load.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Eliminating vasoconstrictor entirely is not the default recommendation for stable
  cardiovascular disease; plain solutions have a shorter duration and higher total anesthetic
  uptake, and adequate pain control itself reduces endogenous catecholamine release from stress.
  Vasoconstrictor-free anesthesia is reserved for more severe, unstable, or specifically
  contraindicated cases.
- C) A higher epinephrine concentration increases cardiovascular stimulant exposure per volume
  injected — the opposite of what is indicated — and 1:50,000 is not recommended for these patients.
- D) A history of significant cardiovascular disease (recent MI, unstable angina, poorly controlled
  hypertension/arrhythmia) requires dose modification and careful technique regardless of how the
  patient feels on a given day; medical consult/clearance is often warranted first.
