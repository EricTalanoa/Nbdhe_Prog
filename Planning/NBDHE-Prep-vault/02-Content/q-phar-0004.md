# Stem
By what mechanism do bisphosphonates such as this patient's medications predispose the jaw to
osteonecrosis, and which exposure carries the higher risk?

# Options
- A) they inhibit osteoclast-mediated bone resorption, suppressing normal bone turnover and healing; the intravenous route carries higher risk than the oral route (correct)
- B) they stimulate osteoclasts to resorb bone excessively, and the oral route is far riskier than intravenous
- C) they thin the blood and cause uncontrolled bleeding after extractions, unrelated to bone turnover
- D) they cause an immediate hypersensitivity reaction in the jawbone independent of dose or route

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Bisphosphonates bind to bone mineral and **inhibit osteoclast function**, reducing bone resorption
and remodeling. Because turnover is suppressed, injured bone (as after an extraction) cannot remodel
and heal normally, and micro-injuries accumulate — the basis for osteonecrosis. Risk is
**dose- and duration-dependent and higher with intravenous** nitrogen-containing bisphosphonates
(e.g., zoledronic acid used in oncology) than with **oral** agents taken for osteoporosis, though
oral therapy over several years still carries measurable risk.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) The direction is reversed: bisphosphonates **inhibit**, not stimulate, osteoclasts; and the
  **IV** route is the higher-risk route, not the oral route.
- C) Bisphosphonates are antiresorptives, not anticoagulants; the jaw problem is impaired bone
  healing, not a bleeding disorder.
- D) MRONJ is a turnover/remodeling problem that is dose- and route-related, not an immediate
  allergic (hypersensitivity) reaction in bone.
