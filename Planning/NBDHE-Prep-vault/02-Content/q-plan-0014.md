# Stem
A dental hygienist about to administer a maxillary infiltration can most effectively reduce a
patient's anxiety about needle insertion by:

# Options
- A) applying a topical anesthetic to dried mucosa at the injection site for the recommended time before penetration (correct)
- B) inserting the needle quickly without any warning, to minimize anticipation
- C) telling the patient the injection "won't hurt at all"
- D) skipping the topical anesthetic to save chair time, since the area will be anesthetized anyway

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
A topical anesthetic applied to dried tissue for the manufacturer's recommended time numbs the
surface and blunts the initial sensation of needle insertion — a simple, evidence-based comfort
measure layered onto local anesthesia technique that directly targets injection-related anxiety.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) A sudden, unannounced insertion increases anxiety and the risk of patient movement; a calm,
  forewarned (tell-show-do style), slow technique is preferred.
- C) Promising "no pain" sets up a false expectation that breaks trust if the patient feels any
  sensation.
- D) Skipping the topical anesthetic removes a proven comfort measure and increases the perceived
  pain of penetration.
