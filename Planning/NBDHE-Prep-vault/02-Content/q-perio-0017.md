# Stem
An essential oil mouthrinse (containing thymol, eucalyptol, menthol, and methyl salicylate)
reduces plaque and gingivitis primarily by:

# Options
- A) disrupting bacterial cell walls and inhibiting bacterial enzymes on contact, with limited oral substantivity compared with chlorhexidine (correct)
- B) binding electrostatically to oral surfaces for 8-12 hours of sustained antibacterial release, the same mechanism as chlorhexidine
- C) selectively inhibiting only gram-negative anaerobic pathogens deep within the periodontal pocket
- D) forming a physical barrier film over the tooth surface that mechanically blocks new plaque attachment

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Essential oil rinses act primarily through direct antimicrobial contact: the phenolic/essential-oil
components disrupt bacterial cell walls and interfere with bacterial enzyme activity, producing a
meaningful reduction in supragingival plaque and gingivitis with regular twice-daily rinsing. Unlike
chlorhexidine, essential oils have comparatively limited substantivity (they are not retained on
oral surfaces for many hours), so their effect depends more on rinse frequency and contact time
than on prolonged surface binding.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Long substantivity (roughly 8-12 hours of sustained release from binding to oral surfaces) is
  the hallmark of chlorhexidine, not essential oil rinses — this is a key distinction tested between
  the two agents.
- C) Essential oils act broadly against the supragingival plaque biofilm; they are not a targeted,
  pocket-penetrating therapy against specific anaerobic pathogens.
- D) Essential oil rinses are chemotherapeutic (antimicrobial), not a mechanical barrier or sealant
  film over the tooth surface.
