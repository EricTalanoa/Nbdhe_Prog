# Stem
Compared with traditional quadrant scaling and root planing spread across several weeks, the
one-stage full-mouth disinfection protocol (all quadrants scaled within 24 hours, combined with
chlorhexidine rinse/gel/pocket irrigation) was developed primarily to:

# Options
- A) reduce the chance that bacteria from untreated pockets recolonize already-treated sites during therapy (correct)
- B) eliminate the need for any follow-up periodontal maintenance visits
- C) allow the clinician to avoid using local anesthesia during instrumentation
- D) replace host-modulation therapy as the primary treatment for aggressive periodontitis

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
With quadrant-by-quadrant scaling spaced weeks apart, pathogenic bacteria in still-untreated
pockets (and on the tongue/tonsils/oral mucosa) can recolonize sites that were already
instrumented, a phenomenon sometimes called intraoral translocation. Treating the full mouth within
a short window, paired with an antiseptic to suppress bacteria at extra-pocket reservoirs, was
designed to reduce this cross-contamination and improve clinical outcomes.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Full-mouth disinfection changes how initial therapy is delivered, not whether ongoing
  supportive periodontal maintenance is still needed afterward.
- C) The rationale for compressing scaling into 24 hours is microbiological (limiting
  recolonization), not a change in anesthesia technique; local anesthesia is still typically used
  for thorough instrumentation.
- D) Host-modulation therapy (e.g., subantimicrobial-dose doxycycline) targets the host inflammatory
  response and is an adjunct in select cases; full-mouth disinfection is a scheduling/antiseptic
  strategy for mechanical debridement, not a replacement for it.
