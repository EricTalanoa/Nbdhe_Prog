# Stem
Immediately after a posterior superior alveolar nerve block, a patient develops rapid swelling and
bluish discoloration over the injection site. This is most likely the result of:

# Options
- A) an IgE-mediated allergic reaction to the anesthetic
- B) inadvertent laceration of the pterygoid venous plexus causing a hematoma (correct)
- C) intravascular injection producing transient facial nerve paralysis
- D) trismus from repeated needle insertions into the masseter muscle

# Correct answer
B

# Rationale
The posterior superior alveolar injection carries a well-known risk of nicking the pterygoid
venous plexus with the needle. Because the area is highly vascular, this produces rapid, visible
swelling and bluish discoloration as blood extravasates into the surrounding tissue — a hematoma.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- A) A true allergic reaction typically presents with urticaria, itching, or respiratory symptoms,
  not localized swelling and bruising at the injection site.
- C) Intravascular injection can cause transient effects like a rapid heart rate or facial pallor,
  but it does not cause facial nerve paralysis or the described bruising.
- D) Trismus is painful limited jaw opening from muscle/tissue trauma, typically from an inferior
  alveolar block; it does not produce visible swelling and discoloration.
