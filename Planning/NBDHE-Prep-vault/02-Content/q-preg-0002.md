# Stem
The patient asks whether her puffy, bleeding gums and the growth between her front teeth mean the
pregnancy is "ruining her teeth." Which explanation and management plan is most accurate?

# Options
- A) Pregnancy hormones exaggerate the gum's response to plaque, so meticulous plaque control is the key treatment, and the reactive growth usually shrinks after delivery (correct)
- B) The changes are caused directly by the hormones alone, so oral hygiene will not affect them and no cleaning is needed until after delivery
- C) The growth is a tumor that must be surgically removed immediately regardless of the pregnancy
- D) Calcium is being pulled from her teeth by the fetus, so the teeth themselves are dissolving

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Pregnancy gingivitis reflects an **exaggerated inflammatory response to existing plaque biofilm**,
driven by elevated estrogen and progesterone that increase gingival vascularity and permeability.
The etiologic factor is still plaque — so **thorough plaque control (plus professional debridement)
is the core of management**, and it is both safe and recommended during pregnancy. The
interproximal "pregnancy epulis" (pyogenic granuloma) is a benign reactive lesion that typically
regresses after delivery once hormone levels fall and plaque is controlled; excision during
pregnancy is reserved for lesions that bleed excessively or interfere with function.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Hormones **modify the response** but do not replace plaque as the cause; saying hygiene won't
  help, and deferring all cleaning, is both wrong and harmful — it lets inflammation worsen.
- C) The epulis is a benign reactive lesion, not a neoplasm requiring urgent removal; the usual
  approach is plaque control and observation, with elective excision deferred unless problematic.
- D) The "pregnancy pulls calcium from the teeth" idea is a myth — enamel is not a metabolically
  labile calcium store; the teeth are not dissolving to supply the fetus.
