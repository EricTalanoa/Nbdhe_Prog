# Stem
Adding sodium bicarbonate to buffer a local anesthetic solution just before injection is intended
to raise the solution's pH closer to the drug's pKa, which:

# Options
- A) increases the proportion of drug in the uncharged (base) form, speeding diffusion across the nerve sheath and onset of action (correct)
- B) permanently deactivates the vasoconstrictor so it cannot cause local ischemia
- C) converts the anesthetic from an amide to an ester, changing how it is metabolized
- D) extends the anesthetic's duration of action well beyond its unbuffered formulation

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Local anesthetic solutions are manufacturer-formulated at an acidic pH (partly to keep the
vasoconstrictor stable), which favors the charged, ionized form of the drug. Buffering with sodium
bicarbonate raises the pH toward the drug's pKa, shifting more molecules into the lipid-soluble,
un-ionized base form that crosses the nerve sheath and axonal membrane more readily — producing a
faster onset and, often, a more comfortable injection.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Buffering does not deactivate epinephrine; it is done carefully and close to the time of
  injection because a higher pH can accelerate vasoconstrictor breakdown if solutions sit too long,
  not because the vasoconstrictor is intentionally disabled.
- C) Buffering changes solution pH, not the drug's chemical class; an amide anesthetic remains an
  amide, metabolized in the liver.
- D) Buffering primarily speeds onset; it does not reliably extend how long the anesthetic lasts,
  which is governed mainly by protein binding and vascularity at the injection site.
