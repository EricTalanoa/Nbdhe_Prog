# Stem
Given this persistent, indurated erythroleukoplakic lesion on the lateral tongue in a heavy
tobacco-and-alcohol user, what is the most appropriate action?

# Options
- A) refer promptly for biopsy to rule out squamous cell carcinoma (correct)
- B) prescribe an antifungal rinse and recheck in three months
- C) reassure the patient it is likely traumatic and take no further action
- D) polish the area and place fluoride varnish over the lesion

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Any oral lesion that **persists beyond about two weeks** without an obvious resolving cause —
particularly one that is **indurated**, has mixed red-and-white change, sits at a **high-risk site**
(lateral/ventral tongue, floor of mouth), and occurs in a patient with heavy tobacco and alcohol use
plus a firm regional node — must be treated as **suspicious for malignancy**. The definitive step is
**referral for biopsy**, the gold standard for diagnosis. Delaying diagnosis of oral squamous cell
carcinoma worsens the prognosis.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Empirically treating as candidiasis and waiting three months delays diagnosis of a possible
  cancer; induration and a persistent ulcer are not features of candidiasis, which typically wipes
  off (pseudomembranous) or responds quickly.
- C) The features here (induration, persistence, high-risk site, no trauma source, firm node) are
  the opposite of a benign traumatic ulcer; reassurance without biopsy is unsafe.
- D) Polishing and fluoride do nothing for a suspected malignancy and inappropriately delay the one
  action that matters — obtaining a tissue diagnosis.
