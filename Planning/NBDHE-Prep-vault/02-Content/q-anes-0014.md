# Stem
A patient with severe trismus from a recent TMJ flare-up cannot open the mouth wide enough for a
standard inferior alveolar nerve block landmark approach. Which injection technique is most
appropriate for anesthetizing the mandibular quadrant?

# Options
- A) Vazirani-Akinosi closed-mouth mandibular block (correct)
- B) Gow-Gates block, using the standard wide-open landmarks
- C) Standard inferior alveolar nerve block, injecting slightly higher than usual
- D) Mental nerve block alone to cover the full quadrant

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
The Vazirani-Akinosi technique is a closed-mouth mandibular block performed with the teeth in
occlusion (or as close to it as the patient can manage), using the mucogingival junction of the
maxillary arch and the height of the mandibular ramus as landmarks instead of a wide-open jaw. This
makes it the technique of choice for patients with limited mandibular opening (trismus, TMJ
disorders, or post-surgical restriction) who cannot achieve the mouth opening a standard IANB or
Gow-Gates approach requires.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) The Gow-Gates technique also requires a wide-open mouth to locate the condylar neck landmark,
  so it is not usable in significant trismus.
- C) A standard IANB depends on visualizing/palpating landmarks reached with a wide-open mouth;
  simply adjusting injection height does not solve the access problem.
- D) The mental nerve block only anesthetizes the lip/chin and facial gingiva anterior to the
  mental foramen — it does not anesthetize the pulps of the mandibular teeth or lingual tissues.
