# Stem
Regarding this patient's lesion, which statement about oral premalignant/malignant lesions is
correct?

# Options
- A) an erythroplakic (red) component carries a higher risk of harboring dysplasia or carcinoma than a purely leukoplakic (white) lesion (correct)
- B) leukoplakia is always more dangerous than erythroplakia because white lesions are keratinized
- C) the dorsum of the tongue and hard palate are the highest-risk intraoral sites for squamous cell carcinoma
- D) a lesion that cannot be wiped off is by definition benign

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Among clinically descriptive lesions, **erythroplakia (a red patch) has a much higher likelihood of
showing epithelial dysplasia, carcinoma in situ, or invasive carcinoma** than leukoplakia, and mixed
**erythroleukoplakia** carries elevated risk as well. The **highest-risk intraoral sites** for
squamous cell carcinoma are the **lateral/ventral tongue and the floor of mouth**, which is exactly
where this lesion sits — reinforcing the concern.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) The opposite is true: a red (erythroplakic) lesion is generally **more** worrisome than a white
  (leukoplakic) one; keratinization of a white lesion does not make it more dangerous.
- C) The dorsum of the tongue and hard palate are comparatively **low-risk** SCC sites; the
  lateral/ventral tongue and floor of mouth are the high-risk locations.
- D) "Does not wipe off" distinguishes a lesion from removable debris or pseudomembranous
  candidiasis, but it does **not** establish that a lesion is benign — many carcinomas do not wipe
  off, which is why biopsy is required.
