# Stem
Using the 2017 AAP/EFP classification, which periodontal diagnosis best fits this patient's
charting, radiographs, and risk profile?

# Options
- A) Generalized Stage III, Grade C periodontitis (correct)
- B) Generalized Stage II, Grade B periodontitis
- C) Generalized Stage III, Grade B periodontitis
- D) Generalized Stage IV, Grade C periodontitis

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
**Staging** is set by severity and complexity at the worst site: interdental CAL of ~7 mm and
radiographic bone loss extending into the **middle third** of the root place this at **Stage III**
(CAL ≥5 mm, bone loss mid-third or beyond, with complexity factors such as Class II furcation and a
questionable tooth) — but not Stage IV, since there is no need for complex full-mouth rehabilitation
from masticatory dysfunction or extensive tooth loss. It is **generalized** because ≥30% of teeth
are involved. **Grading** estimates the rate/risk of progression: destruction out of proportion to
biofilm, plus the Grade-C risk modifiers **smoking ≥10 cigarettes/day** and **diabetes with HbA1c
≥7.0%**, raise the grade to **C**.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Stage II caps at 3–4 mm CAL and coronal-third bone loss; this patient's 7 mm CAL and mid-third
  bone loss exceed Stage II, and Grade B understates the smoking/diabetes risk.
- C) The stage is correct, but the grade modifiers (smoker ≥10/day, HbA1c ≥7.0%, rapid
  biofilm-disproportionate loss) move it from B to **C** — this is the classic point the grade is
  meant to capture.
- D) Stage IV requires additional complexity such as masticatory dysfunction, bite collapse, or
  extensive tooth loss needing complex rehabilitation; those features are not described here.
