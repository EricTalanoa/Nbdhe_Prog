# Stem
While bacterial biofilm initiates periodontal disease, most of the actual collagen and alveolar
bone destruction seen in periodontitis is caused directly by:

# Options
- A) the host's own immune-inflammatory response, including neutrophil enzymes, cytokines, and matrix metalloproteinases released in reaction to the biofilm (correct)
- B) mechanical trauma from normal mastication acting on inflamed tissue
- C) direct enzymatic digestion of bone by periodontal pathogens' exotoxins
- D) osmotic pressure changes caused by increased crevicular fluid volume

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Periodontal bacteria are the initiating factor, but most of the tissue destruction is
host-mediated: the immune-inflammatory response to bacterial antigens releases neutrophil
enzymes, pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-1, TNF-alpha), and matrix metalloproteinases that
break down collagen and stimulate osteoclastic bone resorption. This host-response concept
explains why modifying the host response (e.g., host-modulation therapy) can slow disease
progression even when some bacterial challenge remains.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Masticatory forces are not the mechanism of periodontal tissue breakdown; occlusal trauma is
  a separate, distinct process from inflammatory periodontal destruction.
- C) Periodontal pathogens contribute virulence factors, but the bulk of bone and collagen loss is
  driven by the host's own inflammatory mediators, not direct bacterial enzymatic digestion of
  bone.
- D) Increased gingival crevicular fluid flow is a sign of inflammation, not a destructive
  mechanism itself; it does not directly cause collagen or bone loss.
