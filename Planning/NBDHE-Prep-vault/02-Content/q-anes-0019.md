# Stem
As a local anesthetic takes effect, a patient typically loses pain and temperature sensation
before losing touch, pressure, and motor function in the same area. This order of loss occurs
because:

# Options
- A) smaller-diameter, lightly or unmyelinated nerve fibers carrying pain and temperature are blocked before larger-diameter, more heavily myelinated fibers carrying touch, pressure, and motor function (correct)
- B) the anesthetic is deliberately injected closer to pain fibers than to touch and motor fibers
- C) pain fibers regenerate more slowly than motor fibers, so they appear to be blocked first
- D) touch and motor fibers require a higher total anesthetic volume, not a different blocking mechanism, so they simply take longer to fill the tissue

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Local anesthetics block nerve fibers in a fairly predictable order related to fiber diameter and
degree of myelination: smaller, lightly myelinated A-delta and unmyelinated C fibers (pain,
temperature, and autonomic function) are blocked before larger, more heavily myelinated A-beta
and A-alpha fibers (touch, pressure, proprioception, and motor function). Recovery of sensation
occurs in the reverse order, with motor and pressure sensation typically returning before pain
sensation fully returns.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) A single injection deposits anesthetic around the entire nerve trunk; the differential effect
  comes from fiber properties, not selective proximity to one fiber type over another.
- C) Nerve fiber regeneration is unrelated to the reversible pharmacologic blockade produced by a
  local anesthetic, which does not involve any fiber damage or regeneration.
- D) The differential blockade is explained by fiber diameter and myelination, not by anesthetic
  volume or a diffusion-only mechanism.
