# Stem
Which single feature most reliably distinguishes a malignant neoplasm from a benign one?

# Options
- A) the capacity to invade surrounding tissue and metastasize to distant sites (correct)
- B) a faster overall rate of growth
- C) being composed of epithelial rather than connective tissue
- D) the presence of a well-formed fibrous capsule

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
The defining behavior of malignancy is **invasion and metastasis** — malignant cells break through
basement membranes, infiltrate adjacent structures, and spread via lymphatics or blood to distant
sites. Benign tumors, by contrast, remain localized and expand by pushing tissue aside. This
biological behavior — not size, growth rate, or cell of origin — is what makes a neoplasm
malignant.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Growth rate is variable and overlaps between the two: some benign lesions grow quickly and
  some malignancies grow slowly, so speed alone does not classify a tumor.
- C) Both benign and malignant tumors can arise from epithelium (e.g., papilloma vs. squamous cell
  carcinoma) or from connective tissue; tissue of origin does not determine malignancy.
- D) A well-formed capsule is typical of **benign** tumors, which are circumscribed; malignant
  tumors characteristically lack a true capsule and have infiltrative, ill-defined margins.
