# Stem
Six weeks after completing scaling and root planing for a patient with moderate chronic
periodontitis, periodontal reassessment shows improved anterior probing depths with no bleeding,
but the posterior sextants still show 5-6 mm pockets with bleeding on probing. Which additional
finding would most strongly support referral for surgical periodontal therapy rather than
continued nonsurgical retreatment of those posterior sites?

# Options
- A) Class II-III furcation involvement with continued bleeding on probing despite adequate root debridement (correct)
- B) mild marginal gingival redness with no bleeding on probing
- C) a probing depth of 3 mm with no bleeding on probing in the anterior sextant
- D) the patient reports improved home-care compliance since the initial visit

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Furcation areas are difficult or impossible to fully instrument with closed, nonsurgical
technique. Persistent bleeding and deep pocketing in a furcation despite technically adequate
root debridement is a classic indication for surgical access (e.g., open flap debridement) to
achieve thorough root-surface instrumentation.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Mild redness without bleeding on probing suggests low-grade inflammation, not a treatment
  failure requiring surgery.
- C) A 3 mm pocket with no bleeding indicates periodontal health at that site, not a need for
  further intervention.
- D) Reported compliance is reassuring but does not, by itself, indicate that surgical therapy is
  needed at the persistently inflamed posterior sites.
