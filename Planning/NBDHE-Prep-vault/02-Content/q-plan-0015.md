# Stem
When instructing a caries-active patient on fluoride toothpaste use, the hygienist should advise
the patient to spit out the excess after brushing but avoid rinsing with water immediately after,
because rinsing:

# Options
- A) dilutes and clears the fluoride left on tooth surfaces and in saliva, reducing its remineralizing effect (correct)
- B) reactivates the abrasives in the toothpaste, increasing enamel wear
- C) neutralizes the fluoride ion, converting it into an inactive compound
- D) has no measurable effect on caries risk either way

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Topical fluoride works by remaining in contact with enamel and in saliva after brushing, where it
promotes remineralization and inhibits demineralization. Rinsing with water right after brushing
washes away much of that residual fluoride, so patients are coached to spit, not rinse, to get the
full topical benefit.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) Rinsing does not reactivate abrasives; abrasive action happens during brushing, not after.
- C) Water rinsing dilutes and removes fluoride but does not chemically neutralize it.
- D) Reducing post-brush fluoride contact time measurably lowers the topical benefit, which is why
  this instruction is given specifically to caries-active patients.
