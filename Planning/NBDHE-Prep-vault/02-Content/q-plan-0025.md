# Stem
A patient reports sharp, short-lasting pain from cold beverages localized to a small area of
exposed root surface with no caries or defective restoration present. Along with an in-office
fluoride varnish application, which home-care instruction is most appropriate?

# Options
- A) switch to a desensitizing dentifrice containing potassium nitrate or stannous fluoride and use it daily in place of a whitening toothpaste (correct)
- B) increase brushing frequency to six times daily with a medium-bristle brush to mechanically seal the exposed tubules
- C) discontinue fluoride toothpaste, since fluoride exposure is the most likely cause of the sensitivity
- D) rinse twice daily with a chlorhexidine gluconate mouthrinse until the sensitivity resolves

# Correct answer
A

# Rationale
Dentin hypersensitivity from exposed root surfaces is managed with desensitizing agents:
potassium nitrate diffuses through open dentinal tubules to depolarize the intradental nerve and
reduce its ability to respond to stimuli, while stannous fluoride (and other agents such as
arginine/calcium carbonate) works primarily by occluding the tubules. Whitening dentifrices often
contain abrasives or peroxide that can worsen sensitivity, so switching away from one while adding
a desensitizing formula addresses both the mechanism and a likely aggravating factor.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- B) A medium or firm brush used more frequently increases the risk of further gingival recession
  and cervical abrasion, worsening tubule exposure rather than sealing it; a soft brush with
  gentle technique is indicated instead.
- C) Fluoride toothpaste is not the cause of dentin hypersensitivity and is part of the
  recommended treatment (both varnish and fluoride dentifrice), not something to discontinue.
- D) Chlorhexidine reduces bacterial plaque and gingival inflammation but has no established role
  in occluding dentinal tubules or blocking nerve conduction for hypersensitivity.
