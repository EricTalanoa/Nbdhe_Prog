# Stem
All of the following are recognized mechanisms of fluoride's cariostatic action EXCEPT:

# Options
- A) promoting remineralization by favoring formation of fluorapatite in enamel
- B) inhibiting bacterial enzymes (e.g., enolase) involved in glycolysis, reducing acid production
- C) acting as a bactericidal agent against cariogenic bacteria at sufficiently high, topical concentrations
- D) inhibiting collagenase activity within the periodontal ligament to prevent attachment loss (correct)

# Correct answer
D

# Rationale
Fluoride's three established cariostatic mechanisms are: (1) inhibiting demineralization and
enhancing remineralization by forming the more acid-resistant fluorapatite/fluorhydroxyapatite,
(2) interfering with bacterial carbohydrate metabolism (e.g., inhibiting enolase), which reduces
acid production, and (3) exerting a direct antibacterial/bactericidal effect on cariogenic
bacteria at higher topical concentrations. Inhibiting periodontal ligament collagenase is not a
recognized fluoride mechanism; it is not part of how fluoride prevents dental caries.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- A) This is a true, well-established remineralization mechanism of fluoride.
- B) This is a true anti-metabolic mechanism of fluoride on cariogenic bacteria.
- C) This is a true direct antibacterial mechanism of fluoride at higher concentrations.
