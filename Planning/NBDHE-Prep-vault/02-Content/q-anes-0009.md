# Stem
Local anesthetics produce their clinical effect primarily by:

# Options
- A) destroying the myelin sheath surrounding the nerve fiber
- B) blocking voltage-gated sodium channels in the nerve membrane, preventing depolarization (correct)
- C) competitively binding acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction
- D) causing irreversible protein denaturation within the axon

# Correct answer
B

# Rationale
The uncharged base form of the local anesthetic diffuses across the nerve membrane, where it
re-ionizes; the charged cationic form then binds a receptor site inside the voltage-gated sodium
channel, blocking sodium influx. This prevents the membrane depolarization needed to reach
threshold, so the action potential cannot propagate and the nerve is reversibly blocked.

**Why the distractors are wrong**
- A) Local anesthetics do not damage the myelin sheath; the effect is a reversible channel
  blockade, not structural nerve injury.
- C) Acetylcholine receptor blockade describes neuromuscular blocking agents (e.g., used in general
  anesthesia), not local anesthetics.
- D) The anesthetic effect is reversible and does not involve denaturing axonal proteins.
